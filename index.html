Project #2 Persuasive or Deceptive Visualization?

David Oh — d9oh@ucsd.edu

Proposition: The United States has had a higher gross savings than China until recently.

FOR the Proposition

Design Decisions and Rationale:

Design Decision 1: Use of Absolute Savings

Truthfulness Score: 2

This metric accurately represents the actual gross savings that each country has over time. It directly supports the proposition that the US had more gross savings than China until recently.

Design Decision 2: Line Graph to Clearly Show Data Over Time

Truthfulness Score: 2

I chose to utilize a line graph for its clarity in communicating the data over a period of time. This allows the audience to easily track the point at which the gross savings of China caught up to and overtook the US.

Design Decision 3: Y-axis Scaling

Truthfulness Score: 1

The y-axis doesn’t start at 0, which could be seen as slightly dishonest. However, this emphasizes the differences between the two countries over time. On the other hand, it can also be seen as exaggerating the visual gap

Design Decision 4: Color Encoding

Truthfulness Score: 2

I chose to use blue and orange to distinguish the US and China, respectively. These colors were chosen for their high-contrast and colorblind friendliness.

AGAINST the Proposition

Design Decisions and Rationale:

Design Decision 1: Use of Gross Savings as a Percentage of GDP

Truthfulness Score: -1.5

Although the graph is technically accurate, the visualization utilized gross savings as a percentage of GDP, not total gross savings. This shifts the narrative to suggest that China had more in gross savings the entire time.

Design Decision 2: Same Line Graph and Color Encoding as First Visualization

Truthfulness Score: 2

Again, I am utilizing a line graph for clear communication of the data over time. In addition, I am using easily distinguishable colors to encode the information in the visualization.

Design Decision 3: Lack of GDP Context

Truthfulness Score: -1

There is no real clarification on how GDP is calculated and that a higher % of GDP does not necessarily mean a higher total savings. For some viewers, this may mislead them in interpreting this visualization and believe the deception.

Design Decision 4: Use of Point Markers and Thick Lines to Emphasize Gap

Truthfulness Score: 1.5

The point markets and lines make it easy for the viewer to track year by year changes. This also reinforces the narrative that China had more in savings over time by having the lines having a gap and never touching each other.

Final Reflection

By designing both of these visualizations, it revealed how visual framing and deceptive techniques can be utilized to shape interpretation. Although the data was all true and accurate, the utilization of slightly different metrics (normalized vs not) changed the narrative entirely. This project has highlighted how crucial it is to not only present accurate data but clearly communicating what that data represents. Choices in axis scaling, metric selection, omission of crucial context, and even color encoding can introduce bias, even if that was not the intention. I believe that visualizations should emphasize clarity, accuracy, truthfulness, and especially transparency. On the other hand deceptive visualizations exploit ambiguity or cover up what the data actual represents.
